Kishan Putta 

DC for Democracy Questionnaire

Ward 2 Council - 2020 Democratic Primary

TAXATION

 

1. This chart (source: https://itep.org/whopays/washington-dc/) shows the current tax structure for DC households (including sales, property, and income taxes). Do you think this is a fair tax structure? If so, explain why. If not, explain why and how you would improve it.

What affects one DC resident in one part of our city also affects me and my neighbors. We must all support each other if our city is to improve for all. We must all do our part and contribute our fair share.

Our current tax structure is not fair enough.  We need to scrutinize the current tax credits and reduce or eliminate those that are not benefiting DC residents enough — for example the many millions of dollars of Qualified High Technology Company tax credits for which the Chief Financial Officer was not able to identify any economic benefits.  Tax credits that are not providing proven benefits to DC residents should be reduced or cut so we can better target our tax dollars to those who need help most.

It’s also noteworthy that the wealthiest DC residents pay a smaller percent of their incomes in property taxes than the lowest-income households in DC, and DC’s property taxes are lower than the surrounding areas.

Overall, we need to consider the total taxes (income, property, and sales) that are being paid by each segment of our population and strive to achieve a progressive tax system.

People should generally pay more property taxes as their properties increase in value. I support limits to property tax increases for seniors on fixed incomes. And for those who are not high income, are genuinely struggling with annual increases, and can demonstrate hardship, I would want to look for limited, targeted ways to help them deal with the increases as well.

I would also consider implementing a “teardown tax” similar to the one that is currently being considered in Montgomery County. This tax would be levied when single-family homes get replaced with much larger, much more expensive single-family properties. This proposal is estimated to raise over $100 million over ten years.

WORKERS’ RIGHTS

2. DC has a proud history of passing landmark labor laws but a lackluster record of enforcing these laws or proactively informing workers about existing or new rights. What will you do as a CM to change this status quo and ensure there is strong oversight & enforcement of worker rights?

DC has worked to secure a number of labor laws that mandate extensive protections of all workers. These include raising the minimum wage to $15 by 2020, a vital paid family leave policy, and protections for pregnant workers. These ensure basic dignity for all workers

 

However, when it comes to enforcement, our city has serious work to do. To make these labor laws effective, we need to restore autonomy and power of working people into our economy and democracy.

 

To do so, I would first like to remove barriers that prevent workers from unionizing. Unions play a critical role in protecting employee rights and ensuring that they have full access and knowledge about their pay, benefits, working conditions and protections.  As the Center for American Progress suggests, as Ward 2 Councilmember, I will allow unions to communicate with workers through modern and convenient means, and help educate new public employees about the benefits of union membership.

We also need to take steps to protect our most vulnerable workers, including immigrants, women and LGBTQIA+ workers. Right now, employers have an ability to intimidate their undocumented immigrant workers by threatening to call ICE. This power imbalance results in immigrants sometimes working in unfair conditions that exploits their fear of deportation.

EDUCATION

3. Publicly funded charter schools do not have the same transparency requirements as traditional DCPS schools including disclosing salaries, records, or vendor contracts less than $100,000 nor are charter schools required to have open meetings. Charter advocates argue that they lack the budget to comply with FOIA requests while open government advocates and parents want more access to information. Do you believe charters should be subject to FOIA? How would you resolve these opposing stances on charter school transparency?

Transparency is extremely important in all government work - work that uses and requires taxpayer dollars - especially for services children depend on.

I believe public charter schools would provide better service to our children if they were more transparent. Unlike their traditional counterparts, charter schools are not required to make salaries, records, or vendor contracts under $100,000 public.  They also aren’t required to have open meetings. As councilmember, I will push to change that.

I support Councilmember Allen’s Public School Transparency Amendment Act of 2019.  Under this Act, charters will be required to release FOIA requests to the DC Council.

It won’t be easy for charters to increase transparency--especially to their traditional public school counterparts.  It will take a transition.  And we should support them in that transition.  To aid charters in this transition period, we can help them pool their resources and with some IT support, ease the adminstrative burdens on reporting requirements so that schools are being transparent without impacting their mission to educate - in fact, the transparency should make them more accountable to their missions.

As Councilmember, I will ensure that charters are using their funding effectively and helping our most marginalized school populations become college ready.

Lastly, I would like to mention that my education platform has a strong emphasis on quality neighborhood schools.  Good schools are vital to goals of racial and economic equity and inclusion.  And I have worked hard for them: last year, I was selected as a member of the DC Public Schools Parents Cabinet, where I learned about DC education issues and school financing. I pushed for more funding for schools and for at-risk students and pushed back against funding cuts, even hidden cuts, such as shifting security costs under individual school budgets - cuts which disproportionately hurt low income black and brown students. Funding intended for at-risk students has been misused and is insufficient to help those in need. I support efforts to require DCPS to submit an annual report to the DC Council explaining how each school spends at-risk funding. And this should apply to charter schools as well.

I also believe that we must increase access to educational technology--an issue that greatly affects racial and social equity. That’s why I worked so hard with parents citywide to successfully advocate for $4.6 million in tech support and over 15,000 new, quality, reliable tech devices for DCPS students. They should be reaching students by December. I will continue pushing this next year with parents citywide to reach a 1:1 ratio of one device per DCPS student. Many school systems have done this or are on track to reach this. It is an important equity issue and a priority of mine. It will help so many students and families who can’t afford these vital devices themselves.

HOUSING

4. DC4D has joined the Reclaim Rent Control coalition, which advocates for expanding rent control. Do you support their platform in its entirety? If not, explain where you differ. What other ideas would you propose to help residents who are negatively impacted by rising rents?

So many residents depend on rent control. I know how vital it is. I support the core components of the Reclaim Rent Control platform, and I am a strong advocate for the reauthorization of DC’s rent control law. My thoughts regarding the three components of the platform are as follows:

  1. Expand rent control to protect more tenants:  I support expanding rent control to more units. I believe the most impactful way to do this is via incremental change. Expanding rent control can also include properties that were built before 2005 or include 4-unit buildings - but should be done incrementally with a transition based on data for how best to transition. I strongly agree with allowing only one rent increase per year for all DC rental units as suggested in the platform.

  1. Close loopholes in the existing law to stop the loss of affordable housing: I fully support all subcomponents of associated with this part of the platform.  

  1. Keep rent-controlled housing affordable for current tenants:  I agree with eliminating the vacancy incentive for landlords. I agree that keeping rent-controlled housing affordable for current tenants is of the utmost importance. In order to make sure that DC’s rent-controlled housing is high quality and accurately meets health and safety standards, I support keeping CPI + 2%, rather than 0%, so that landlords have a revenue stream for making important health and safety improvements.


Increase the housing supply, especially affordable housing:  I also think it is imperative that we take action to increase the housing supply--especially affordable housing--by building new units.  A recent study by the Urban Institute recommends 374,000 new units of housing region-wide by 2030 to close the affordability gap and meet the market demands.  So far, I’ve proposed the following solutions:

  • Allow individual homeowners to build Accessory Dwelling Units (ADUs) on their property. I have had experience and success advocating for ADUs in Ward 2. These ADUs of course have to pass inspection and be safe for tenants and would be subject to the rules/restrictions on short-term housing. But such changes could incentivize hundreds of homeowners to create affordable units.
  • Building more housing above retail spaces. There are several locations that could work. Just one example is the Safeway on Wisconsin Ave. It should have had housing built on it, and maybe it still can. Just a block away, another grocery store recently opened with housing above. Both are on a major transportation corridor. There are many other such opportunities in Ward 2. It is important that such developments have good access to existing transit or that more transportation options be made available to serve more people.
  • On DC-owned land in and near Ward 2, the District should consider building more affordable units.
  • As telecommuting, co-working, and gig-economy work rises, and traditional office space becomes underused, I would like to incentivize building affordable housing units in these spaces. Others have proposed using vacant office space in Ward 2 for affordable childcare. Both are important affordability priorities that can potentially both be served well and co-located in certain cases.
  • I would also support incrementally allowing more rowhouses and small apartment buildings near major transit corridors in Northwest DC, while simultaneously beefing up transit options and availability and reliability, including dedicated bus lanes - something I’ve been pushing for almost a decade.

5. Our public housing tenants are facing a serious crisis, and we have little confidence in the DC Housing Authority’s plan to “reposition” public housing. DC4D has endorsed a petition to DCHA with recommendations on protecting tenants. Have you signed the petition; if not, why not? What other policies would you propose to protect and/or expand public housing and housing for very low-income residents in DC?

Maintaining public housing is one of my top priorities, and I will work tirelessly to ensure that we not only improve the quality of public housing but also increase the amount of housing in Ward 2.  Residents of all income levels should have the opportunity to find housing and keep our city accessible for diverse groups of people. This should be a citywide goal.  Having represented and been a homeowner in two different parts of Ward 2, I understand the challenges and am committed to more affordable housing in Ward 2.

To get this done, we must increase funding for the Housing Production Trust Fund.  But, we also need more transparency and accountability to make sure that the Fund is maximally impactful.  Too many projects have been approved that don’t provide enough housing for the most needy. Many more units could have been created with the same funding and I am committed to doing more with our precious housing funds. Strong oversight would include ensuring that site visits are executed, and that sites are submitting their required annual certifications.

We also need to utilize an important tool: rent control. All families and people deserve high-quality, affordable homes. I support reauthorization of rent control as it currently standing and further protecting the rights of tenants. A critical part of this is eliminating loopholes that are misused by landlords such as hardship petitions. This has enabled properties to become dilapidated. This is unacceptable, and is something that I will work to change as Councilmember.

Greenleaf Gardens absolutely needs drastic reform. I fully support rebuilding the public housing into safe, healthy, and dignified high-quality units. While rebuilding, we need to make sure that the current tenants are protected, displacement will be minimized, and D.C. Housing Authority has a clear plan for rebuilding these units. The reposition plan as it stands does not ensure that tenants are protected.

GOVERNMENT/ETHICS

6. Are you participating in Fair Elections? If not, why not? What other ideas would you propose to limit the influence of money in politics?

YES - I actively advocated for the Fair Elections Program and proudly supported the efforts of Councilmember Charles Allen and others. Councilmember Allen even tweeted a photo of me testifying before him on the issue (alongside leaders of JUFJ and DCWFP!).

I am proud to pledge that I will never have my decision-making influenced by big money.  If elected, I will work to ensure the longevity of the Fair Elections program. That is one of the reasons I hope to join the Judiciary Committee as councilmember.

I have always believed that the biggest problem underlying politics is campaign finance. I have long been in support of changing the incentives through public financing (or something like the “democracy dollars” program in Seattle). Removing barriers for residents to run for office is one of the best ways to encourage underrepresented voices in public office.

That is why I talk about the Fair Elections Program often. Sometimes residents question this use of tax dollars. But I tell them what I told the council:  our current system results in too much corporate welfare and unwarranted tax breaks that got enacted because of the skewed incentives corporations benefit from under the campaign finance system. Fair Elections will curb those incentives and likely save much more money than is spent - so we can spend our tax dollars more wisely to benefit all DC residents - especially those who need help the most.

7. Do you support abolishing Constituent Service Funds and replacing them with appropriated funds to serve resident needs? If not, explain.

Constituent service is a calling for me.  I have a passion for helping fellow DC residents in need. I’ve worked to help residents in need through 6 years with DC Health Link as well as several years as a locally elected Commissioner in two different neighborhoods of Ward 2 — Dupont/Logan as well as Burleith/Georgetown/Hillandale.

I do support abolishing Constituent Service Funds. I have always felt this way, but even more so after reading the shocking March 2019 report by Public Citizen.

While some elected officials have done some good things with these funds in the past, I believe the best way for officials to help constituents is by pushing the very government that they oversee. I agree that government agencies can/should act faster for residents in a crisis. For select services and agencies, I would work to streamline operations, to get speed up responses in crisis situations (utilities being shut off, eviction support, referrals to social services, referrals to counselors and case workers, etc.), and publicly-funded social services organizations can also help fill gaps in the social safety net.

But for the last 6 years I worked to help vulnerable residents and I know we need to  strengthen our social safety net. There should be more funding to help support residents in need and in crisis - our social safety net has too many holes for far too many to fall through.

For the last 6 years I helped people in danger of being evicted or even dying if they did not get their health care needs covered - quickly.

Every week I received calls from councilmembers about constituents in need. Or from parents about uninsured adults children.  Or from residents about their neighbors in crisis. I heard so many stories about people on the edge.

I have been working on constituent services across Ward 2 for most of the last decade. It is important to work and it is hard work. It takes persistence and experience to do it well and effectively in different areas with different needs.

I handled constituent services in Dupont Circle Logan Circle area and now I handle constituent services in the Burleith area. I hold a monthly conference call with constituents and send monthly newsletters. I am prepared to start serving Ward 2 residents on Day One.

8. Will you commit to vote for Vote16DC?

I am firmly committed to the bold, progressive, and inspiring work done by the Vote16DC advocates. That is one of the reasons I hope to join the Judiciary Committee as councilmember.

I strongly supporting this bill for a number of reasons. First, studies have shown that by lowering the voting age, children are more likely to remain politically engaged into adulthood. A more informed electorate, overall, benefits public policy. Additionally, Vote16DC promotes community unity. As students become engaged at an earlier age, students start paying attention to pressing issues facing their neighborhoods. Finally, turnout is one of the strongest proponents of lowering the voting age; the youth vote is stronger and more consistent than other age voting blocks.

As related note, I support proposals like Councilmember Nadeau’s to allow voting by mail, like Rockville just did successfully! That is one of the reasons I hope to join the Judiciary Committee as councilmember.

9. Since DC frequently has races with many candidates in which the winner gets far less than a majority of the votes, would you support implementing ranked choice voting (RCV) or some other method to ensure some level of majority support?

Ranked Choice Voting offers a truer sense of expressing democratic wishes.  As a supporter of RCV, I was glad to see New York City’s elections to switch to RCV in local primaries, joining several other cities and states. I will push for RCV here in the District. That is one of the reasons I hope to join the Judiciary Committee as councilmember.

I am a firm proponent of a representative, democratic government. Ranked Choice Voting offers us a path towards that goal.  That is why I support Councilmember Grosso’s recently introduced Ranked Choice Voting Amendment Act of 2019.  

Implementing RCV also improves the democratic nature of the election process in more ways than one: it also extends a hand to community members who have become disenchanted with the voting process, thereby increasing voter participation in DC elections.  As Councilmember Grosso pointed out, voters will truly be free to mark the candidate on the ballot that they truly prefer without fear that their vote is completely meaningless.  Voters will not need to choose who they think is the most electable candidate--rather, they can choose the candidate that they feel best represents them and their voice.

10. What role the does DC Council play during the Ballot initiative process?

As the legislative branch of the City’s government, the DC council must be active and available to its constituents. But, I believe the council should also help provide the platform and arena for social change. And when a ballot initiative meets all legal and regulatory criteria and passed by popular vote, I believe councilmembers should abide by the outcome. It is fair to consider whether the process could be improved and clarified. But I believe in honoring the will of the voters of the District of Columbia.

That is why I was appalled that the successful ballot initiative calling for DC officials to have term limits was overturned.

I support term limits and will support them on the council.  I am the only candidate to pledge to self-impose term limits: two terms. I admired Councilmember Grosso for doing this and I would do the same after 8 years of service to--as he put it--“pass the baton to a new generation of progressive leaders.”  We have very few full-time elected officials in DC and I believe that more turnover would be helpful to bring new ideas and perspectives.

CRIMINAL JUSTICE

11. Do you support decriminalizing sex work? If not, explain how you would reduce the violence and exploitation currently suffered by these workers?

I support decriminalizing sex work and Councilmembers Bonds, Grosso, and White’s efforts to pass the Community Safety and Health Amendment Act of 2019.  By removing criminal penalties for sex work, DC can afford these workers basic human rights that protect them from exploitation, such as the ability to report violence on the job to the police without the fear of arrest.

Importantly, the criminalization of this work disproportionately targets some of DC’s most marginalized populations, including trans women of color. I am one of the only candidates running that has experience in doing work that these workers from unjust police practices.  In 2014, I was a commissioner on the Dupont Circle ANC, representing the historic LGBTQ hub of DC: 17th Street NW.  I cosponsored a resolution to repeal a police anti-prostitution tactic that disproportionately targeted the LGBTQ community, especially our trans neighbors.  

We must do our best to protect the vulnerable--and that is why I support this bill.  All people, regardless of their line of work, deserve equal protection of the laws, and from law enforcement.  I understand the concerns that this bill may promote or increase human trafficking.  But that is not what the Community Safety and Health Amendment Act legalizes.  Under the Act, coercing someone to engage in sex work is still illegal, as is human and child trafficking.  Rather, the bill aims to protect the workers, and ensure that they are able to report violence as it happens without fear of arrest.  

12. Do you support ending stop and frisk in DC?

Stop and frisk searches are dehumanizing.  They are cruel and unjustifiable excuses by the police force to stop and harass our most vulnerable residents (often black and brown neighbors, low income residents, LGBTQ neighbors, and immigrants).  I fully oppose stop and frisk policing and will oppose them as councilmember. I have pushed for better police policies in the past and this is one of the reasons I hope to join the Judiciary Committee as councilmember.

As councilmember, I will push DC to learn from other cities across America, like Philadelphia and New York City, which have already curbed stop and frisk searches.  I will work to do the same.

 

I believe the NEAR Act is an excellent first step towards improving policing, but we have more work to do.  This past June, after the ACLU of DC initiated suit against the MPD (the DC police force), a local court ruled that the MPD was in violation of the NEAR Act’s stop and frisk data collection requirements.  Recently, the DC Police finally released this long-awaited data, confirming what my black and brown neighbors have long told me: police stops in DC disproportionately impact minorities.  This is unacceptable.  All DC residents deserve to feel safe in their home—especially our black and brown neighbors who have long been unjustly targeted by the police force.

 

Our police must reform their practices to ensure that they are not further perpetuating racial inequity in DC.  I fully support police reform--and, unlike most other candidates, I already have experience in pushing the police to do more.  In 2015, I accepted an appointment to be a member of the MPD Community Engagement Academy.  As a member of the Academy, I regularly worked with DC’s most senior public safety officials, including the Deputy Mayor for Public Safety and the Chief of Police.  I worked to encourage police officers to engage in cultural competency training, and to think harder about how they police hate crimes.  Further, through my role serving on the Mayor’s Commission on Asian and Pacific Islander Affairs, I worked with Councilmembers Nadeau and Cheh for legislation to push for an MPD Special Liaison Unit on religious minorities.  As Councilmember, I will work just as hard to ensure the police reform their stop and frisk practices.  I promise to help educate the police on why these practices are racist and inhumane, and why they must come to an end.

13. What public health policy ideas would you propose to reduce violence in DC?

The NEAR Act:  I have always believed in leveraging community engagement to make DC a better place--that’s why I am a two-time ANC Neighborhood Commissioner, and why I worked for six years as a Community Outreach Coordinator for DC Health Link to implement President Obama’s Affordable Care Act across our city.  By harnessing the strength that communities have, I believe that we can create long-term, sustainable, and positive change from within.  And that’s why I support the NEAR Act.

A public health initiative that rejects old school “tough on crime” policies, the NEAR Act prioritizes community engagement as a primary strategy to improve neighborhood safety.  If elected, I would work to fully fund the NEAR Act and ensure that we rely less on top-down police enforcement, and prioritize engaging communities to police themselves.  As Councilmember Nadeau wrote earlier this year, we must acknowledge that for many, especially our neighbors of color, increased police presence doesn’t make everyone feel safer.  Rather, as a study by the American Journal of Public Health concluded earlier this year, it’s the opposite: more police contact leads to increased trauma and anxiety.  By creating initiatives like the Office of Neighborhood Safety and Engagement, the Office of Violence Prevention and Health Equity, and community safety walks, the NEAR Act is a step in the right direction.

That said, in order to be fully effective, the NEAR Act must be fully funded.  As Councilmember, I promise to work hard and prioritize funding the Act.

Reducing Recidivism: The issue of recidivism--especially given its cyclical, and thus self-perpetuating, nature--is particularly concerning given its disproportionate impact on communities of color.  We must understand the factors that contribute to and exacerbate DC's high rates of recidivism.

Recent research shows that expansion of healthcare reduces rates of recidivism.  And though this research is only a few months old, I’ve long been an advocate of promoting and expanding healthcare as a means to improving the quality of a person’s life.  As a DC Health Link employee, I worked with returning citizens to make sure they are supported and provided services as they return to society after serving their sentences.

I also would support funding and implementation of the Entrepreneurship Program for Returning Citizens, a program that the DC Council failed to fund in 2017.  The program provides a number of services for returning citizens: networking opportunities; leadership training; mentorship; and workshops for returning citizens who have or plan to start businesses.  Importantly, the Program also recognizes that education can help re-integrate returning citizens, as it provides scholarships for returning citizens who hope to attend college in DC.  And as noted by the Council for Court Excellence, such education is what returning citizens need to successfully find a job in DC: by 2020, it is projected that 76% of all jobs will require postsecondary education.  Yet, only 36.2 percent of men and 36.5 of women incarcerated in DC have less than a high school education.

I believe in second chances--especially for those who committed crimes in their youth. That is why I support the goals of the Second Look Act: it offers a promising solution to end the persistent inequities that exist in our criminal justice system, including those engendering from over-zealous sentencing in the 80s and 90s. The law would require judges to consider a long list of factors when reviewing an individual’s sentence including medical and mental health reports, evidence of rehabilitation and maturity, prison disciplinary records, and victim impact statements. This aspect of the law helps ensure that the law is implemented with care.

Fixing the School to Prison Pipeline:  Good schools are the backbone of a successful community: they are central to achieving racial and economic equity.  As a member of the DCPS Parents Cabinet, I have learned a lot about school financing, and have pushed against cuts that disproportionately hurt our most at-risk school populations—including hidden cuts that shifted security costs under individual school budgets. I support requiring DCPS to submit an annual report explaining their use of funding for at-risk students so that we can hold DCPS accountable for how it uses these funds.

We also must think harder about reforming school discipline policies--especially those that engender the racial discipline gap.  This is particularly relevant in DC, as Black students in DC are almost 12x more likely to be disciplined compared to white students.

 

We also must close the opportunity gap. Leveler schools have been proven to help close the gap, according to the DC Policy Center, but not enough students are able to access them. Long commutes, wait lists, and space constraints all limit access.  As Councilmember, I will make it a priority to ensure that at-risk students have easy access to leveler schools.

IMMIGRATION

14. Do you support legislation to prohibit the District from cooperating with federal immigration agencies, including by complying with detainer requests, absent a judicial warrant or order?

I support legislation prohibiting the District from cooperating with federal immigration agencies. I believe that DC needs to take greater action to protect our immigrant population. This means protecting students in schools and educating the police force and other government agencies about restricting compliance with federal immigration agencies. Keeping our immigrants safe is a priority.

No one should live in fear. I proudly stand by Charles Allen’s bill that limits DC’s cooperation with ICE. This bill is an important step in the right direction, but we need to do more.

Approximately one in seven adult immigrants do not participate in public benefit programs often because they are too afraid of the government - that is not right.

What's more, DC Healthcare Alliance, DC’s healthcare program for immigrants, is inaccessible and confusing. I personally helped many immigrants get health insurance through this program.

But only half of participants apply for renewal when they are eligible. Lack of healthcare and other public benefits has detrimental and painful effects on the health and well being of this immigrant population.

This must change. As council person, I will launch an education campaign to educate city workers on the limited ways in which they have to cooperate with ICE. This importantly includes protecting our students in schools to make sure that their records are never released to ICE agents. It also means opening up a dialogue and continuing education program with the police force.

 

ENVIRONMENT

15. Given Pepco’s resistance to transitioning to clean renewable energy, do you think DC would benefit from municipally owned electricity? If not, why?

Utilities should serve the customer and their communities: they should be affordable, high-quality, and preserve the natural environment.  

It is disturbing that our power utility, Pepco, was approved to merge wwith Exelon approved to become the largest utility provider in the country--a process that went without full transparency to the public. My constituents have come to me with their concerns that Pepco has too much influence in politics, while also prioritizing profits and not customers.

In my neighborhood, we deal with Pepco often when powerlines go down during storms, etc. Many constituents say they are upset that the Exelon merger was approved without enough scrutiny and that they went back on their rate cuts. They believe Pepco has too much influence on our government. That is why I signed the pledge by 350DC - as councilmember I will only meet with Pepco if they agree to full transparency about any such meeting and what was discussed.

Yes, I support the Council conducting an economic feasibility study on municipally owned electricity. I think such a study should examine: the implications of converting Pepco and Washington Gas into public utilities, the implications of a Community Choice Aggregation model in DC, and any other options proposed. We must conduct thorough analysis, talk to all relevant stakeholders, and investigate all potential avenues so we can find the most equitable and fair solution for our residents.

16. What specific legislative initiatives will you introduce in Council Period 24 to protect DC from the effects of climate change?

Experts and organizations all over the world have told us that we have until 2030 to cut half of all fossil fuel emissions so that we can slow the rise of global temperatures and stop the dangerous ramifications of climate change.

My son will be 11 by 2030. He will have to live his entire adult life dealing with the consequences of our actions. It is my responsibility--and our responsibility--to make sure we give our children the best world possible.

That means fighting for a DC run 100 percent on renewable energy. I am proud to support the Clean Energy DC Act, one of the most ambitious clean energy bills in the country. By implementing this Act, we will halve greenhouse gas emissions by 2032. I also strongly support the Green Bank set up by this act - an initiative that reduces the upfront costs of building clean energy projects.

But more needs to be done. I also support pushing for our bus fleets to run fully on cleaner electric power. And I support making it easier to install solar panels on your home. Climate change is an existential crisis and we must do all we can to address it.

HEALTH

17. The DC Council passed the landmark “Birth to Three” legislation providing universal child care at subsidized rates; however, they failed to fully fund it. What solutions would you propose to create sustainable funding for the program?

I have been working on affordable childcare since 2013 - five years before having my first child. As an ANC Commissioner, I fought to get DC to open four new affordable early childhood education programs - parents pay less that $200 per month. But we need to do much more.

As a father with a young son who attends daycare, I know how important it is for families to have access to high-quality, affordable childcare.  Countless studies continue to show the vital impact of investing in a child’s earliest years.

Yet for too many in DC, quality child care is hard to find and expensive. That’s why full funding and full implementation of the Birth-to-Three for All Act is so essential. Cultivating the creation of affordable daycare and early education programs fosters a number of benefits for DC families: it allows parents to begin or return to work; it promotes healthy development, thereby helping avoid interventions, such as special education and grade repetition, later in life; and, over time, it can help close the achievement gap between children of color and white children. DC families deserve this investment into the lives of our young children—and that begins with supporting the implementation of the Birth-to-Three for All Act.

Finding funding and creating dedicated funding for this will be one of my top priorities. I will fight to claw back as much of the online gambling revenue as possible. And I will scrutinize all tax credits for effectiveness, and reduce or cut ineffective or unnecessary tax credits.

Lastly, on affordability generally: one proposal that my constituents brought to me is to create an official DC task force on overall DC affordability issues.  They say that so many different things are becoming unaffordable in different ways and that we need to look at the issue holistically.  And I agree. Such a taskforce would bring together all stakeholders—including lawmakers, policymakers, advocates, and residents—to work on various aspects of affordability, such as housing, childcare, food, transportation, education, etc.  It would advise policymakers to, as much as possible, not tackle these problems separately, but rather in a coordinated fashion so that we aren’t letting one problem get worse while focusing on another.

BACKGROUND

18. (Ward 2 challengers only) Are you committed to removing the incumbent from office? If yes, what criteria should be used to determine who is the best candidate to challenge him?

When I speak with constituents in my neighborhood, they tell me often that they want a new councilmember and are embarrassed by the current incumbent. They tell me they want someone different. The say they want a councilmember who:

  • Brings needed diversity to the DC Council - all current councilmembers are either white or black in a city with tens of thousands of brown people and immigrants of color;
  • Is not tied to the incumbent;
  • Has experience living in or working with the diversity of different neighborhoods of Ward 2;
  • Can relate to the diversity of households in our ward and city: students, singles, couples, parents, seniors, etc.

And I agree with them.  Ideally, the council should be representative of the population it serves. A more diverse council ensures that all voices are represented at the table. Additionally, a track record of results and constituent-based initiatives is crucial to determining which candidate can get things done while serving the public.

For the record, there has never been an Asian-American elected to the DC Council and there are no immigrants of color (or children of) on the council.

19. (Challengers only) As a first-year legislator, you won’t be allowed to chair a committee but will be able to express preferences for which committees you will sit on. Which committee is your top preference and why?

My top preference is to sit on the Committee on the Judiciary and Public Safety which will hopefully still be chaired by Councilmember Charles Allen, who I admire greatly. The committed oversees the Metropolitan Police Department, the Board of Elections, the Office of Campaign Finance, and many other agencies.  

I want to make the police more accountable and better trained and I want to improve our city’s democratic life. As mentioned earlier in this questionnaire, I want to push for:

  • Local voting age of 16
  • Ranked Choice Voting
  • Voting by Mail
  • Preserving and funding the Fair Elections Program

I already have ample experience pushing the the MPD to be better: In 2015, I accepted an appointment to be a member of the MPD Community Engagement Academy.  As a member of the Academy, I regularly met with and pushed back on DC’s most senior public safety officials, including the Deputy Mayor for Public Safety and the Chief of Police. I pushed to change training for police officers to include cultural competency training, and training on hate crimes.

Further, through my role serving on the Mayor’s Commission on Asian and Pacific Islander Affairs, I worked with Councilmembers Nadeau and Cheh for legislation to push for an MPD Special Liaison Unit on religious minorities. Much of the increase in hate crimes in recent years is attributable to religious hate, primarily against Muslims and Jews. After the Charlottesville protests, antisemitic grafitti appeared in my neighborhood and I responded immediately.  

 

Some told me they didn’t want to report it and upset people, but I knew it was important—especially at such a tense time—to show our neighbors that we support them. It was also covered in the Washington Post:  

Kishan Putta, a Georgetown resident on the city’s Commission on Asian and Pacific Islander Community Development, reported the incident to police after neighbors told him about it. He said he thinks the crime was intended to intimidate Jewish residents in the neighborhood. He is hosting a community meeting Thursday to discuss the crime with neighbors. “I worry that this was intended to scare and intimidate them, especially at this tense time... It’s beyond disgusting and scary.”

 

The LGBTQIA+ community has experienced far too many hate crimes, especially the trans community. In 2014, I was a commissioner on the Dupont Circle ANC, representing the historic LGBTQ hub of DC: 17th Street NW.  I cosponsored a resolution to repeal a police anti-prostitution tactic that disproportionately targeted the LGBTQ community, especially trans neighbors. I am a person of color who is opposed to police profiling. It is dehumanizing.  So, I didn’t just cosponsor and vote.  I also made the time to testify before the DC Council about it.

 

Earlier this year, I testified about MPD and how they should make body-worn-camera footage available without charge to ANC Commissions that vote to request it. Councilmembers can do that. And I will.

20. Briefly explain what systems you have or would put in place to get feedback from your constituents on policy?

I am a strong advocate for government accountability, both by the government itself but also by the public.  Too often, we see our public officials conduct backroom deals, with little or no public comment or hearing period, only to show a final deal that is obviously beneficial to the few lucky ones in the room.

I already have made accountability and feedback a foundation of my elected career.  As an ANC Commissioner, I hold monthly conference calls for my ANC constituents who can’t always come to the ANC meetings in person.  

And as councilmember, I will hold monthly town halls in-person and with a live-stream as well for those who can’t attend in person (like parents or seniors or people traveling for work, or otherwise occupied).  Additionally, the video and audio will be archived for those who are not available and they can at least watch or listen at a later time and stay informed of issues, concerns, the Council’s efforts, and my policy proposals — and most importantly, to hear from residents about their ideas and opinions on policies and issues.

I would also have regularly-scheduled monthly calls or meetings with progressive advocates to hear their updates and ideas and opinions. I would also come to their meetings to hear from their members directly.

I also hope to create a DC Council Petition website similar to President Obama’s We The People White House Petitions. DC residents could create a petition to voice their concerns or frustrations with specific issues or bills.  And if the number of signatures reaches an established threshold, it will mandate the DC Council to respond.

Recently I co-authored my first online petition for fair sharing of public facilities - specifically the Jelleff Field. It has almost 3,000 signatures and not only did Mayor Bowser meet with us about it, but the DC Council held a big hearing about it as well. We are still fighting for justice, but the petition was a valuable tool and I believe it should be formalized in our government as President Obama did. I certainly would personally respond to any DC petition with thousands of DC signatures.
