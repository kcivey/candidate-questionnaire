Jordan Grossman 

DC for Democracy Questionnaire

Ward 2 Council - 2020 Democratic Primary

Please respond to dcfodemocracy@gmail.com by November 8. You can answer within the document under each question.

TAXATION

 

1. This chart (source: https://itep.org/whopays/washington-dc/) shows the current tax structure for DC households (including sales, property, and income taxes). Do you think this is a fair tax structure? If so, explain why. If not, explain why and how you would improve it.

I don’t think this is a fair tax structure because it puts an inequitable burden on lower and middle-income residents. I believe we’re at an inflection point in the District: we’re at risk of becoming a place where only the very wealthy can afford to live, at the same time that the wealthiest 5 percent of residents are paying a lower share of their income in taxes than the middle 50 percent. But when I talk to Ward 2 residents, it’s clear that many of us don’t want to keep going down this path. We can make choices now to help ensure we have a more progressive and equitable city in the years and decades to come: meaning a place that is diverse, inclusive and welcoming to people of all incomes, races, and backgrounds.

 

To me, the best way to build a more equitable city is by making structural changes that address the basic needs of residents rather than effectively leaving individual families to fend for themselves. As Ward 2’s councilmember, I will focus on these kinds of structural changes that will make it easier for all people – from fifth generation DC residents like me to our immigrant communities and other new arrivals – to stay and thrive in the homes and neighborhoods that they love. That means taking concrete steps to make it possible for everyone in DC to afford a place to live, find high-quality child care for their kids, walk, bike and take public transit all across the city, and pay off student loan debt – which disproportionately disadvantages Black and Brown families and exacerbates the racial wealth gap. That also means implementing a fairer tax structure by repealing or clawing back the corporate tax breaks that have been championed by Jack Evans for years – and that cost the District tens of millions of dollars a year – and exploring new funding sources that involve the wealthiest paying their fair share.

WORKERS’ RIGHTS

2. DC has a proud history of passing landmark labor laws but a lackluster record of enforcing these laws or proactively informing workers about existing or new rights. What will you do as a CM to change this status quo and ensure there is strong oversight & enforcement of worker rights?

Many members of the DC Council too often treat passing a law or holding a press conference as the end of the process, rather than the beginning. In my career in public service over the past decade, including working in DC’s Medicaid agency, DC’s federal trial court, the U.S. Senate, and two different federal agencies, I have learned the vital importance of continued oversight and accountability to ensure that laws and protections on the books actually make a difference in the day-to-day lives of residents. I have also learned how effective oversight can simultaneously protect residents and save money. For example, I was awarded the Department of Justice Civil Division’s Perseverance Award for my work during the Obama Administration on an investigation that not only resulted in a strong, enforceable corporate integrity agreement to protect patient safety but also returned nearly $155 million to taxpayers. Similarly, my oversight efforts when I worked in the U.S. Senate not only helped protect health care coverage for low-income people benefitting from a little-known provision of the Affordable Care Act, it also forced the Trump Administration to provide millions of dollars to the states choosing to use it.

Rather than the current practice of some members of the Council to wait for a tragedy, a news story, or the executive branch – or all three – to spur action, I would fight for proactive oversight of worker protections from my first day on the job. For example, the Council should, as a matter of course, proactively plan for accountability hearings and check-in mechanisms for every significant deadline included in labor (or other major) legislation that becomes law. If necessary, I would fight to include triggers and claw back provisions to ensure compliance not only with these deadlines and requirements, but also with open or unaddressed recommendations from the Offices of the Inspector General and DC Auditor.

I believe the Council should also enact a mechanism to review the use, or lack of use, of all the enforcement, oversight, and accountability tools it has provided to the executive branch over the years. Although DC “has some of the strongest labor laws on the books” and the Council passed the Wage Theft Prevention Amendment Act in 2014, the DC Just Pay Coalition has found “wage theft remains pervasive” and the Department of Employment Services “is not using the full extent of the law to ensure workers are fully paid” or effectively deterring bad faith employers “from being out of compliance with this or other labor laws.” One can imagine similarly lackluster implementation or enforcement may take place with respect to the Universal Paid Leave Amendment Act, particularly in light of recent proposed regulations that would inappropriately narrow the number of workers who could receive benefits. In short, the Council should explicitly provide for systematic and proactive assessments of the use and effectiveness of the enforcement tools it has passed into law to ensure that they are actually being put to their intended use to improve the lives of workers in the District. If they aren’t, the DC Auditor or other reviewing bodies should provide specific recommendations to the Council for how to make sure they do.

EDUCATION

3. Publicly funded charter schools do not have the same transparency requirements as traditional DCPS schools including disclosing salaries, records, or vendor contracts less than $100,000 nor are charter schools required to have open meetings. Charter advocates argue that they lack the budget to comply with FOIA requests while open government advocates and parents want more access to information. Do you believe charters should be subject to FOIA? How would you resolve these opposing stances on charter school transparency?

The use of public dollars should be accompanied by public accountability. This includes applying the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) equally to charter schools, which educate nearly half of DC students. I found EmpowerEd and the Washington Teachers Union very compelling on these points at the April DC4D meetup. If elected, I would support Councilmember Charles Allen’s Public School Transparency Amendment Act to equalize the transparency requirements between the traditional and charter public school sectors. I have not seen persuasive evidence that doing so would result in an overwhelming financial or administrative burden for charter schools in the District. In fact, the National Alliance for Public Charter Schools lists compliance with freedom of information statutes in its model laws and has said that “in most jurisdictions, the public can attend charter-school board meetings and request records from individual schools.” To resolve opposing stances on this issue, I would be open to adding a provision to Councilmember Allen’s bill requiring the Council to reevaluate the policy after a set amount of time if reliable evidence emerges showing that equalizing transparency requirements is having major negative impacts on DC students.

HOUSING

4. DC4D has joined the Reclaim Rent Control coalition, which advocates for expanding rent control. Do you support their platform in its entirety? If not, explain where you differ. What other ideas would you propose to help residents who are negatively impacted by rising rents?

I support most of the sensible reforms proposed by the Reclaim Rent Control Coalition. First, we should not be providing an incentive for landlords to push tenants out of their units in the form of vacancy increases; if anything, landlords should be incentivized to retain longtime tenants. Second, hardship petitions should be permitted only where absolutely necessary by making them narrower, more tailored, and temporary. Third, we should implement stronger oversight of all petitions for exemptions from rent control, including better and more frequent audits and requiring proof of general compliance with the housing code as a prerequisite. Fourth, building eligibility for rent control should be tied to a dynamic date rather than staying the same for decades on end. Finally, landlords for market-rate housing should be restricted to one rent increase a year to strengthen baseline protections for tenants who do not currently benefit from rent control. That said, I do not support capping annual rent increases at the level of inflation and eliminating the additional 2 percent allowed under current law. Other states that have recently enacted strong rent control reforms – such as California and Oregon – have allowed for rent increases above inflation. I’m concerned that this well-intentioned provision of the Reclaim Rent Control Coalition platform could lead to insufficient maintenance and upgrades at rent-controlled buildings.

In addition to reauthorizing and strengthening rent control, we must take additional actions to help residents struggling with our housing affordability crisis. First, we should press for significant investments in housing that low-income and middle-income residents can afford. As the State of the Capital Region 2019 report noted, “the region added almost twice as many people as housing units” from 2010 to 2017, “housing values have risen faster than income” across our area, and “rising housing costs have regressive economic impacts.” Further, as National Low Income Housing Coalition research makes clear, we need to dedicate particular attention to extremely low income families, who comprise nearly a third of DC renter households.

To do so, the Council should press for substantially increased investments in the Local Rent Supplement Program and the Housing Production Trust Fund, “DC’s primary tool to finance the production and preservation of affordable housing,” and enact more effective requirements for ensuring that units produced with Trust Fund dollars are affordable for low-income residents and families. As Ed Lazere of the DC Fiscal Policy Institute has said, housing "is 3 percent of the city’s budget” but “it’s way more than 3 percent of the city’s problems.” This is also a prime example of an area that deserves more robust Council oversight. As the DC Fiscal Policy Institute has noted, the Trust Fund “is required to devote at least 40 percent of its resources to” extremely low-income households, but “the target requirement has been met only once in five years.” Additionally, the DC Auditor recently found that the Department of Housing and Community Development’s failure to follow its procedures resulted in “a net loss of 353 affordable units, including a reduction by 95 units of those targeting the District’s most vulnerable households earning up to 30 percent of the Area Median Income (AMI).”

I also strongly support other tenant protections to preserve DC’s deeply affordable housing stock, including strengthening the Tenant Opportunity to Purchase Act (TOPA) and implementing the District Opportunity to Purchase Act (DOPA) where tenants are unable to exercise their TOPA rights. Moreover, as I mentioned at the recent Ward 2 candidate forum co-hosted by DC4D, I strongly support the REAR Act, which would require the District to examine racial equity considerations in budgeting decisions and performance metrics. The racial equity assessments required by the REAR Act could be particularly helpful in ensuring that we:

  • Address structural racial inequities that result from laws or policies that appear neutral on their face but actually disparately disadvantage people of color – especially at a time when the Supreme Court and the Trump Administration are seeking to undermine or destroy such race-conscious policy approaches at the federal level;
  • Produce new housing in a way that prevents displacement of long-time residents, especially at a time when our city is experiencing “the greatest ‘intensity of gentrification’ of any in the country;”
  • Press the Department of Housing and Community Development to prioritize compliance with the Fair Housing Act and the principles of the Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing rule—an Obama-era effort to address residential segregation that the Trump Administration has repeatedly attempted to undermine; and
  • Provide support for achieving specific and enforceable targets for each ward for affordable housing efforts to address the clearly inequitable distribution of housing across the District.

Another tool the Council should use to help those struggling with rising rents is the Comprehensive Plan, the “framework that guides future growth and development” in DC. I strongly support the Council including Councilmember Brianne Nadeau’s amendment to make affordable housing, prevention of displacement, and the right of existing residents to return to new on-site units the explicit top priority in the Plan. Current regulations do not set priorities with respect to the list of potential public benefits for development projects, which range from affordable housing to “superior landscaping,” and do not include prevention of displacement whatsoever. As Ward 2’s councilmember, I would fight to send an explicit and enforceable message to developers, courts, and agencies like the Zoning Commission that affordable housing and preventing displacement should be the most important objective in guiding development decisions and priorities in DC in the years to come.  

The Council should also aggressively use its oversight powers to ensure that DC’s existing housing protections are enforced. The District has some of the strongest protections in the country on the books, but they are not a reality for many tenants in their day-to-day lives. The recent tragic deaths of two DC residents, including a child, in a fire make the urgency of stronger oversight and accountability regarding tenant protections heartbreakingly clear. According to a Washington Post investigation “of the city’s handling of the code violations at the property . . . virtually every relevant regulatory mechanism of the city government appears to have failed” these two victims, who lived in “life-threatening housing conditions.”

Similarly, despite the fact that DC law prohibits discrimination against tenants who rely on housing vouchers, enforcement has been so sporadic that some landlords literally advertise in writing that they do just that. While Attorney General Karl Racine has launched admirable efforts to address this issue, the Council should dramatically boost its own oversight of local housing laws. Specifically, the Council should invest in additional housing inspectors and demand stronger oversight (and conduct stronger oversight itself) to ensure that landlords are maintaining safe and livable units and do not exploit or unjustly evict tenants. These efforts could be implemented fairly quickly at relatively low cost, would help identify areas where misspent tax dollars could be recovered and/or redirected, and – most importantly – would put the day-to-day experiences and welfare of DC residents front and center.

Finally, we must do more for the many Ward 2 residents experiencing homelessness – including treating them as our neighbors, not as a nuisance. I support a “housing first” approach with wraparound services and strengthening and expanding outreach teams and day services centers. As the Way Home Campaign has pointed out, it “costs less money for the District to end chronic homelessness than it does to manage it.” Ultimately, the best solution to homelessness is also the best solution for those struggling with rising rents: affordable housing.

5. Our public housing tenants are facing a serious crisis, and we have little confidence in the DC Housing Authority’s plan to “reposition” public housing. DC4D has endorsed a petition to DCHA with recommendations on protecting tenants. Have you signed the petition; if not, why not? What other policies would you propose to protect and/or expand public housing and housing for very low-income residents in DC?

Yes, I have signed the petition. Preserving or rehabilitating existing housing is one of the quickest and least expensive ways we can address housing affordability in DC. This is an especially important priority for longtime residents who are at risk of displacement from their homes and communities as housing and other costs continue to increase. Remedying the deplorable state of public housing in the District that's literally making children, the elderly, and other residents sick is a particularly urgent priority. In short, public housing residents, as well as other District residents struggling with skyrocketing housing costs, deserve “safe, healthy and dignified” housing that they can afford.

I support maintaining as much public housing as possible. Where residents cannot feasibly remain in public housing in light of the federal government’s systematic disinvestment and the severe lack of maintenance these facilities have received over time, alternative plans must directly address “the barriers and discrimination that DCHA voucher holders face when trying to rent in the District, as well as the barriers former public housing residents face when trying to return to the mixed-finance properties currently in DCHA’s portfolio.” This includes prioritizing a “build first” approach to minimize or avoid temporary displacement and emphasizing project-based vouchers or rental assistance that guarantees long-term affordability and stability. As Empower DC, Bread for the City, Washington Legal Clinic for the Homeless, and several other advocates who organized the petition referenced above have noted, alternative plans must also recognize and accommodate not only “the years of harm endured by public housing residents who have been living in slum conditions,” but also the important support systems and communities that many public housing residents have developed over the years.

GOVERNMENT/ETHICS

6. Are you participating in Fair Elections? If not, why not? What other ideas would you propose to limit the influence of money in politics?

Yes! The first step to limiting the influence of money in DC politics is defeating Jack Evans. Ward 2 needs a councilmember who doesn’t work as a lobbyist and sell his services to influence our government at the same time that he is serving as our elected representative. Evans has literally promised in writing to leverage his “network of business relationships developed as an elected official” for paying clients. The recent independent report commissioned by the DC Council confirmed that he did exactly that, including taking hundreds of thousands of dollars from undisclosed private clients who had business before the city. Enough is enough.

Participating in Fair Elections is enabling us to build a powerful grassroots campaign that has the resources and capacity to take on the wealthy corporations and well-connected insiders that have long supported Evans and often dominate District government. I’m proud to lead the Ward 2 field by a wide margin in Fair Elections donations – overall and from DC residents – with the vast majority of our contributions coming from people who have never donated to a DC Council candidate before.

I also support banning councilmembers from outside employment, fully funding and implementing the District’s new clean elections law to ban pay-to-play campaign contributions, and refusing contributions from fossil fuel corporations or their lobbyists as part of the No Pepco Pledge. But we need to go further. We should prohibit councilmembers from ever lobbying the DC Council on behalf of for-profit entities, even after they leave office. We need to stop the revolving door culture at the Wilson Building, where former elected officials so often become lobbyists – sometimes even before their terms are up. These reforms will help restore public trust and begin to address structural issues that disproportionately disadvantage low-income residents and communities of color.

7. Do you support abolishing Constituent Service Funds and replacing them with appropriated funds to serve resident needs? If not, explain.

Yes. Constituent Service Funds should not be used by councilmembers like Jack Evans to pay parking tickets or spend more than $162,000 on seats at sporting events. As Public Citizen has described, Constituent Services Funds as currently structured are “a backdoor way for corporate entities and the wealthy to curry favor with lawmakers.” In fact, as several media outlets and the recent independent report commissioned by the DC Council have noted, this is exactly how Jack Evans has used his Constituent Service Fund – he explicitly asked his lobbying clients to donate to his Fund at the same time they were paying him to circumvent government oversight. It’s time to abolish Constituent Service Funds and replace them with appropriated funds to be used exclusively for residents’ immediate needs, such as medical emergencies, utility payments, rental assistance, and funeral support.

13. What public health policy ideas would you propose to reduce violence in DC?

As Ward 2’s councilmember, I would support policies to prevent violence and incarceration in the first place. I believe we should expand the Office of Neighborhood Safety and Engagement and Attorney General Karl Racine’s Cure the Streets program, which use “proven, public-health strategies that treat violence like a disease that can be interrupted, treated, and stopped from spreading.” This includes recruiting “violence interrupters” who have deep roots in their neighborhoods to intervene before disputes escalate into violence – and before law enforcement needs to get involved.

I also strongly support the full and transparent implementation of the Neighborhood Engagement Achieves Results (NEAR) Act. Unfortunately, the NEAR Act is at risk of becoming an example of a dynamic that happens far too often on the DC Council, where members think their work is done after they pass a law. As I’ve learned from a decade in DC government and all three branches of the federal government, when a bill is passed, the work has just begun. Whether it is making benefits easier to access through a program that will affect nearly half of DC residents at DC’s Medicaid agency, implementing the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act and the Affordable Care Act (ACA) at the Department of Health and Human Services, or conducting oversight of the Trump Administration’s attempts to limit funding for states that chose to use a little-known provision of the ACA to help low-income residents in the U.S. Senate, I have extensive experience fighting to carry out laws as intended – including years after passage.

As a member of the Council, I will push for regular, transparent, and proactive NEAR Act oversight. The Council’s efforts here should involve a continuous process of examination, progress reporting, and improvement rather than seeking simple yes-or-no answers about the implementation of individual provisions. This kind of approach is particularly important for achieving the law’s overarching objectives of meaningfully shifting our criminal justice system to focus on violence prevention and addressing troubling racial disparities in policing. Such disparities have been confirmed as a result of the District finally beginning to comply with the NEAR Act’s data requirements. We should use this data to help guide reform efforts to prevent many of the unjust arrests and convictions that lead to incarceration. The NEAR Act also promotes community policing, which emphasizes officers developing relationships and building trust in the neighborhoods they patrol and can result in better outcomes for both law enforcement and the community. The law also includes important provisions to divert those with mental health needs who encounter law enforcement to treatment rather than incarceration.

I also support the Second Chance Amendment Act, which would allow those convicted of a crime committed at a young age to have a judge review their sentence after they have already served 15 years. More and more research has shown and the U.S. Supreme Court has recognized that many young people do not fully develop their judgment and decision-making capacities until their 20s. As Attorney General Racine and Councilmember Charles Allen have said, “it’s vital to our health and public safety that we foster and reward those who rehabilitate from a serious offense” by “following the science, research and data” embodied by this legislation.

Additionally, one of the many reasons I strongly support DC statehood is that the District deserves the right to guide our own criminal justice system in a more progressive direction. In the interim, I support efforts like those of Congresswoman Eleanor Holmes Norton to regain control of elements of the local criminal justice system incrementally, such as the organization of the District’s court system and the selection of judges. The District should also pursue federal legislation to shift our parole system back to local control from the U.S. Parole Commission. According to the Washington Lawyers’ Committee for Civil Rights and Urban Affairs, the U.S. Parole Commission “helps drive mass incarceration in DC” by “not releasing people on parole who arguably should be released from prison” and “revoking parole or supervised release (and re-incarcerating returning citizens) for minor technical violations.” What’s more, the sentences for these minor technical violations “are much harsher than the sentences issued by the former D.C. Board of Parole.” Additionally, keeping DC inmates in federal custody often means they are hundreds of miles from home, straining family and community ties that are essential to avoiding recidivism. Further, federal authorities often fail to coordinate with the DC government when releasing those in its custody. None of this federal involvement in our local affairs actually helps reduce violence in DC.

Finally, we must improve the systems and processes for connecting returning citizens to the DC services for which they qualify, from enrolling in housing assistance and Medicaid to receiving job training and other supports. As I saw when I worked for a DC agency that is making it easier for all residents to enroll in these kind of benefits, returning citizens can face particularly difficult challenges navigating the bureaucracy. As a member of the DC Council, I would focus on ensuring that efforts like the recently established READY Center, which is intended to serve as a “one-stop shop” for these services and supports, are making a meaningful difference. Reconnecting returning citizens with family and friends, seamlessly enrolling them in the services they need to get back on their feet, and facilitating smooth re-entry into the community and the workforce will go a long way to creating a safer DC for all.

IMMIGRATION

14. Do you support legislation to prohibit the District from cooperating with federal immigration agencies, including by complying with detainer requests, absent a judicial warrant or order?

Yes. I also support legislation to increase funding for attorneys to help undocumented DC residents. More broadly, the District should conduct a top-to-bottom review of all data sharing with the federal government to address other ways in which immigrant families and neighbors may be vulnerable. For example, the Trump Administration’s cruel attempts to punish immigrants who use basic government services – such as the proposed “public charge” or “mixed status” rules – affect key DC health, housing, and other benefit programs that involve extensive collaboration with the federal government.

We must do better when it comes to locally funded programs as well. DC implemented a rule in 2011 requiring participants in the Healthcare Alliance program, which primarily serves immigrants who cannot otherwise obtain health care coverage, to recertify their eligibility every six months rather than once a year like most other programs. When I oversaw a division of the District’s Medicaid agency, I saw the harms of this rule firsthand, as participants often must miss work and wait for hours on end to avoid losing their health insurance. I strongly support eliminating this unequal treatment.

ENVIRONMENT

Yes. Councilmember Mary Cheh proposed a study of municipally owned electricity in 2015, but – as the recent independent report commissioned by the DC Council showed – Jack Evans successfully defeated it in concert with Pepco, a lobbying client of the law firm he joined. This demonstrates why we need to get corporate money out of our politics in order to build a more sustainable future in DC. That’s what the No Pepco Pledge is all about, and it's why I am proud to have been one of the first candidates to sign on to it. The pledge includes a commitment to “support legislation for an economic feasibility study on the implementation of alternative utility models such as Community Choice Aggregation and municipalization in DC,” in addition to rejecting campaign contributions from and disclosing all meetings with Pepco, Washington Gas, and other fossil fuel companies. If I'm elected, I'll make decisions about environmental policy based on the merits, not based on contributions from fossil fuel corporations or secret meetings with their lobbyists.

16. What specific legislative initiatives will you introduce in Council Period 24 to protect DC from the effects of climate change?

First and foremost, I will engage in proactive oversight to ensure effective and transparent implementation of the Clean Energy DC Omnibus Amendment Act that was signed into law in January. This is “the most ambitious clean energy law in the nation” and I will support additional legislation needed to meet its goals, such as bills to fund clean electric buses and push WMATA to electrify its bus fleet.

Second, I will propose legislation to dramatically improve bus service to provide a frequent, reliable, and affordable alternative to driving in all parts of DC in order to build a more sustainable future. Consistent with the Washington Area Bus Transformation Project’s recommendations, we need to run buses more often on as many routes as possible, give buses priority on roadways, establish more dedicated bus lanes on major arteries throughout the area, complete these projects much more quickly, set clear and specific targets for frequency, reliability, safety, and affordability, and empower local and regional officials to achieve a truly integrated regional transportation system.

Third, I would support sensible reforms to strike a better balance between historic preservation and the existential threat of climate change. For example, we should work to make it possible for people to install removable solar panels on most homes without having to undergo extensive bureaucratic reviews or endure arbitrary decisions of the Historic Preservation Review Board (such as a recent denial because “this vision of a row of houses with solar panels on the front of them … just — it upsets me”). I would also support efforts to direct the Historic Preservation Review Board “to balance the needs of a growing city with their mission of protecting DC’s historic resources.” Not all historic buildings or districts warrant a maximal level of preservation, and we should permit more flexibility to accommodate efforts to mitigate climate change, among other important goals.

Fourth, I plan to support DC Water’s Clean Rivers Project “to build 18 miles worth of tunnels” to divert rainwater and sewage across the District to a treatment facility instead of it flooding neighborhoods and getting dumped, “untreated, into local rivers.” This will become ever more critical in DC as we experience more frequent and more intense flooding as a result of climate change – like we did in July.

HEALTH

17. The DC Council passed the landmark “Birth to Three” legislation providing universal child care at subsidized rates; however, they failed to fully fund it. Would solutions would you propose to create sustainable funding for the program?

Budgets are about priorities. If elected, one of my top priorities would be investing in affordable, high-quality child care and our early education workforce. Just as the District found a way to fund universal pre-K, we can and should fully fund the Birth-to-Three for All DC Act to address major racial and economic disparities, pay our early education workforce fair wages, and ensure no family pays more than 10 percent of their income just to have a safe place for their children to learn.

One of the biggest challenges that I hear about from Ward 2 families is the cost of child care, which averages $23,666 a year in DC – about $10,000 more than in Virginia and $8,700 more than in Maryland. As the Birth-to-Three Policy Alliance has noted, our current underinvestment in early childhood education “intensifies racial and economic inequities in DC.” Lack of access to high-quality, affordable child care also forces many women out of the workforce or prevents many women from obtaining higher-paying jobs, particularly women of color. These dynamics affect the early education workforce itself as well, “as the District’s early educators are largely women of color who must support their own families on meager wages.” Child care workers in DC earn an average of $29,450, an income that is thousands of dollars less than what is considered an “extremely low income” household in DC.

To ensure that the Birth-to-Three for All DC Act is fully funded and implemented, I support allocating surplus revenue, redirecting current spending, and exploring new dedicated funding for it. Specifically, the District’s Chief Financial Officer (CFO) recently revised its estimates to show nearly $80 million in additional revenue in fiscal year 2019, $32 million in fiscal year 2020, and millions more by fiscal year 2023. This is an important potential source of funding for Birth-to-Three. The CFO has also found that the tax breaks available to Qualified High Technology Companies (QHTCs) cost the District “$40 million in foregone revenue every fiscal year” and has concluded that “a significant portion of the dollars claimed have gone to companies that were already in the District without any evidence of increased benefits to the District.” I would support redirecting some or all of these funds to funding Birth-to-Three. More broadly, I would also support enacting claw back provisions that apply to all corporate tax breaks in DC so that companies that fail to comply with their responsibilities or that abandon the District must pay back what they received and/or face additional financial penalties. Additionally, the DC Auditor has estimated that the District’s convention and sports authority, Events DC, will have nearly $180 million in excess reserves above and beyond what is needed for all existing commitments in fiscal year 2020. Events DC has also made several questionable spending decisions, such as sponsoring a second division Welsh second team and paying for an esports-themed bar. As a councilmember, I would explore any available options to redirect these dollars to Birth-to-Three and other priorities that will make the District a more progressive and equitable place for residents and their families. Moreover, if the extremely problematic sports betting contract remains on the books, I will fight for half of all revenue to go to Birth-to-Three as initially promised. Finally, I would explore ideas for an additional dedicated funding source for Birth-to-Three.

BACKGROUND

Yes! I believe the most progressive and viable candidate who represents a clean break from Jack Evans and his corrupt machine should replace him. Criteria to determine who is the best candidate could include which candidates have demonstrated the strongest grassroots support in the Fair Elections system (for example, the shortest time to qualify for Fair Elections, the highest number of DC donations, the largest total amount raised, and/or the highest number and proportion of new donors brought into the process who have never donated to a DC Council campaign before); which candidates have earned the support of key progressive organizations; and which candidates have previously donated to or served in leadership roles on Jack Evans’ prior campaigns or associated organizations.  

19. (Challengers only) As a first-year legislator, you won’t be allowed to chair a committee but will be able to express preferences for which committees you will sit on. Which committee is your top preference and why?

The Committee on Housing and Neighborhood Revitalization is my top preference because the District faces a housing crisis – and the DC Council needs to use all the tools at its disposal to address it. Our housing crisis affects everyone in Ward 2, from those renting their first apartment, families who need more space for a new baby, longtime residents who can’t afford to stay where they’ve lived for decades, to our neighbors experiencing homelessness. While many members of the DC Council acknowledge the crisis, recent budgets and committee priorities don’t seem to actually treat it like one. Housing may represent "3 percent of the city’s budget,” but “it’s way more than 3 percent of the city’s problems.”

As outlined in Question #4 above, I believe the Council and the Committee should confront the housing crisis by substantially increasing investments in the Local Rent Supplement Program and the Housing Production Trust Fund; enacting and enforcing more effective requirements for ensuring that units produced with Trust Fund dollars are affordable for low-income residents and families; pressing the Department of Housing and Community Development to prioritize compliance with the Fair Housing Act and the principles of the Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing rule; preserving or rehabilitating the District’s existing affordable housing stock, including public housing; dedicating particular attention to extremely low income households, which comprise nearly a third of DC renter households; dramatically boosting oversight of local housing laws to ensure that the protections the Council has already enacted are actually making an impact in the day-to-day lives of residents; reauthorizing and strengthening rent control; ending chronic homelessness; rejecting the unnecessary tax breaks for new luxury developments that Jack Evans has championed for years; and making affordable housing, prevention of displacement, and the right of existing residents to return to new on-site units the Comprehensive Plan’s explicit top priority.

20. Briefly explain what systems you have or would put in place to get feedback from your constituents on policy?

When it comes to policy, I believe everyone should have a seat at the table and decisions should be made in transparent processes with clear rules, not in secretive backroom meetings with just a few wealthy corporations and well-connected insiders. 

To make that vision a reality, I would establish office policies to ensure that we hear from every constituent who wishes to share their thoughts and concerns;  convene regular working sessions for all Advisory Neighborhood Commissioners in Ward 2 to collaborate on issues and hear feedback; and conduct active and persistent outreach to involve Ward 2 residents who work nontraditional hours or to populations whose voices often go unheard – like those experiencing homelessness. Canvassing and other campaign tactics to meet residents where they are should not stop on Election Day. As a councilmember, I will continue to have meet and greets, knock on residents’ doors, and ask for constituent feedback at Metro stations, farmers markets, and other community events. I will also host “office hours” in every neighborhood in the ward. I believe that it’s on us as public servants to find a way to reach as many residents as possible, including those who feel disconnected from the political process or who – due to financial, family, or other obligations – can’t come to the Wilson Building on a regular basis.

I will also ensure that other key communities and stakeholders throughout the District are involved in policymaking. That means ensuring unions and working families have a prominent voice in decisions about establishing a fair minimum wage, tax policy, and paid family leave. That means restoring voting rights to residents who have been involved in the criminal justice system and lowering the voting age to 16. That means signing the No Pepco Pledge – refusing contributions from fossil fuel corporations or their lobbyists and being open and transparent about meetings with them. And that means robust implementation of campaign finance reforms like Fair Elections and the District’s new clean elections law to ban pay-to-play campaign contributions from those seeking large government contracts.

At the end of the day, I believe that being an effective councilmember means fighting for inclusion so that we have a government where decisions are based on the policy merits and the lived experiences of all our constituents, not the narrow preferences of those who can cut the biggest checks.
