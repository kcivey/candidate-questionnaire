Daniel Hernandez 

DC for Democracy Questionnaire

Ward 2 Council - 2020 Democratic Primary

TAXATION

 

  1. This chart (source: https://itep.org/whopays/washington-dc/) shows the current tax structure for DC households (including sales, property, and income taxes). Do you think this is a fair tax structure? If so, explain why. If not, explain why and how you would improve it.

- This is not a fair tax structure, and we need to keep this in mind as we evaluate various revenue streams. We should not see the drop in income share that we see here with the top 5%.

The vast majority of what drives this discrepancy given the generally progressive income tax structure are the regressive sales and excise taxes and property taxes.

To improve it, I think we can keep in mind a few things. I think one of the first areas that we can consider is the Mansion Tax proposal by the DC Fiscal Policy Institute (DC FPI).

Particularly as we discuss sales and excise taxes like the soda tax, we need to consider the disparate impact and consider offsetting them under our income tax structure.

EDUCATION

  1. Publicly funded charter schools do not have the same transparency requirements as traditional DCPS schools including disclosing salaries, records, or vendor contracts less than $100,000 nor are charter schools required to have open meetings. Charter advocates argue that they lack the budget to comply with FOIA requests while open government advocates and parents want more access to information. Do you believe charters should be subject to FOIA? How would you resolve these opposing stances on charter school transparency?

- Charter schools absolutely should be subject to FOIA and open government laws. They are publicly funded institutions and should be subject to the same transparency rules as DCPS.

If this requires a change in the funding model, I would be open to consider that, though I’d like to see real evidence of a need.

HOUSING

4. DC4D has joined the Reclaim Rent Control coalition, which advocates for expanding rent control. Do you support their platform in its entirety? If not, explain where you differ. What other ideas would you propose to help residents who are negatively impacted by rising rents?

- I support a number of the reforms, but I can’t commit to the platform in its entirety at this time. My stances on a number of the proposals:

- I support eliminating vacancy increases, which I believe encourage predatory landlord practices.

- I support eliminator voluntary agreements, which I believe encourage predatory landlord practices.

- I support the CPI+0% rate increase reform.

- I support the restriction on the frequency of rent increases.

- I support the stronger audits of landlord petitions, and the capital reserve and housing code compliance requirements.

- I support the listed restrictions on capital improvement petitions.

- Further, I support efforts to increase enforcement of habitable living standards. I support increasing the number of housing inspectors and ensuring that the Attorney General’s office has sufficient staffing to pursue landlords who are not maintaining their properties in habitable conditions.

- On the other items, I’d like to see additional information on the possible impacts as I’d be concerned about their long-term impact to housing in the District. It’s essential that we approach this correctly and do not create unintended consequences that future generations need to resolve.

5. Our public housing tenants are facing a serious crisis, and we have little confidence in the DC Housing Authority’s plan to “reposition” public housing. DC4D has endorsed a petition to DCHA with recommendations on protecting tenants. Have you signed the petition; if not, why not? What other policies would you propose to protect and/or expand public housing and housing for very low-income residents in DC?

- I have signed the petition. Given the massive scale of the problem, I think our best path forward is a build-first replacement model with mixed-income developments that at a minimum retain the affordability makeup of what’s being replaced. The affordability of the replacement development should be guaranteed in perpetuity.

With the replacement developments being mixed income, I think this also helps ensure that we’re never in a situation like we are today, where the problems have been ignored for decades and many people are living in truly deplorable conditions.

- Other policies I support would be efforts to increase housing production and require or encourage a greater portion of that to be dedicated to low- and middle-income housing. Job growth in the District continues to drastically outpace housing growth, and we need to address that disparity and make up for the decades of pushing out low income residents.

13. What public health policy ideas would you propose to reduce violence in DC?

- I strongly support expansion of the violence interrupter work under ONSE and the AG’s Cure the Streets program.

- We need to ensure people are healthy and have food to eat.

- We need to expand mental health services, particularly in our schools.

- We need to consider the various factors that lead to violence. We need to provide hope and options to people. We need to provide job training and skill development. We need to ensure our children have a safe and positive environment to learn in.

As we discuss how we make progress, I think it’s important to have elected officials who understand the challenges people face and how they end up on these paths to crime and violence (in addition to the things that should not be crimes).

I grew up with some that managed to escape poverty and violence, and some that didn’t. I’ve lost people to substance abuse, suicide and gun violence. I have deep, personal experience with the issues we need to address and overcome. I believe this experience will be immensely valuable as we seek to improve the lives of our residents.

ENVIRONMENT

15. Given Pepco’s resistance to transitioning to clean renewable energy, do you think DC would benefit from municipally owned electricity? If not, why?

- Definitely! Electrical supply is a natural monopoly, and any service that is a natural monopoly we need to consider municipal ownership or other reforms or requirements of private service.

People must buy power service and we need to ensure there are no predatory practices taking advantage of the lack of competition. There are clear advantages to the provider being an organization that has a commitment to service and the public rather than profit and investors.

My favorite example of this is the Chattanooga Electric Power Board (EPB), which is one of the top-rated utilities by consumers in the country according to Consumer Reports.

In addition to their excellently rated electricity service, when Chattanooga was facing serious issues of Broadband Internet access from the incumbent Internet Service Provider (ISP), the EPB stepped up and provided fiber internet service to their service area, which is also rated extremely well.

This is an excellent example of what’s possible when there’s a commitment to the public rather than profits.

16. What specific legislative initiatives will you introduce in Council Period 24 to protect DC from the effects of climate change?

- I will push for legal adoption of the MoveDC plan. Greater bus, bicycle, and transit usage will help reduce greenhouse gas usage in the District.

- I will further support efforts to ensure our construction and land management is climate resilient.

HEALTH

17. The DC Council passed the landmark “Birth to Three” legislation providing universal child care at subsidized rates; however, they failed to fully fund it. Would solutions would you propose to create sustainable funding for the program?

        - I’d like to start with evaluating DC FPI’s Mansion Tax proposal.

- I also support a thorough review of our tax expenditure programs. An initial rule here could be to only support tax expenditures that the DC CFO deems beneficial to the District and an economically good idea. Unnecessary tax subsidies are too often given to corporations.

- As we discuss full funding of the legislation, we should also understand and evaluate the economic benefits of early childhood assistance. I believe legislation like this provides a long-term cost reduction to the government as well as unlock economic potential, leaving more money for consumer spending and greater possibility for both parents to be able to afford to work.

BACKGROUND

18. (Ward 2 challengers only) Are you committed to removing the incumbent from office? If yes, what criteria should be used to determine who is the best candidate to challenge him?

- Yes. I think voters should determine who they think is the best person to improve DC. In the presidential primary, my criteria is who I think is the best candidate to move our country forward. We shouldn’t accept anything less locally.

19. (Challengers only) As a first-year legislator, you won’t be allowed to chair a committee but will be able to express preferences for which committees you will sit on. Which committee is your top preference and why?

- Oh, this is difficult. I’m very interested in the Committee on Business and Economic Development, or whatever becomes of the now removed Finance Committee, as I’d like to see reforms or better decisions on many tax and business development policies.

I’m also very interested in the Committee on Transportation and the Environment as we’re simply not moving fast enough on bike, bus, transit, and street safety issues in the District.

But if I can only pick one, I’d choose the Committee on the Judiciary and Public Safety. We need an aggressive voice for policing and criminal justice reform, not to mention Council ethics rules and election reforms.

20. Briefly explain what systems you have or would put in place to get feedback from your constituents on policy?

- I will remain accessible to the community. I intend to continue playing recreational soccer in the ward, and I will always invite the community to come chat and provide their feedback.

- Additionally, I will maintain a series of office hours at various locations and times across the ward, providing additional opportunities for constituents to be heard.

- Further, I will ensure that my staff are properly tracking, responding to, and escalating feedback and requests from constituents.

- To provide notice, I will maintain a newsletter and encourage constituents to subscribe. I will also evaluate other outreach and informational channels, such as social media and presentations at community gatherings.
