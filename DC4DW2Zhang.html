Yilin Zhang 

DC for Democracy Questionnaire

Ward 2 Council - 2020 Democratic Primary

TAXATION

 

1. This chart (source: https://itep.org/whopays/washington-dc/) shows the current tax structure for DC households (including sales, property, and income taxes). Do you think this is a fair tax structure? If so, explain why. If not, explain why and how you would improve it.

DC pays more than any state per capita in federal taxes. It would be valuable to look at local taxes in conjunction with federal taxes, to comprehensively assess how much each income bracket is paying in total taxes.

Before making changes to the tax structure, we should better understand cause and effect (e.g., if a tax decrease on one group and increase on another will enhance the current state and not have unintended negative consequences) and assess whether the money can be derived from the current budget. Funding should be going to priority and near-term initiatives.

The tax structure (and any incentives) needs to be structured in a way that does not place an unfair burden on low-income households, and, at the same time, promotes diverse economic development (e.g., encourages small business owners to establish their businesses in DC and stay, and hire locally). It also needs to be mindful of potential unintended consequences.

WORKERS’ RIGHTS

2. DC has a proud history of passing landmark labor laws but a lackluster record of enforcing these laws or proactively informing workers about existing or new rights. What will you do as a CM to change this status quo and ensure there is strong oversight & enforcement of worker rights?

If elected as a CM, several actions are important to the enforcement of worker rights. This includes, but not limited to:

  • Maintain an open line with communication with community leaders, advocates, and non-profits.
  • Collaborate and build relationships with the appropriate agencies, in addition to oversight.
  • Ensure that our laws are proactive. It is important to continuously identify gaps in current legislation and potential unintended consequences. 

EDUCATION

3. Publicly funded charter schools do not have the same transparency requirements as traditional DCPS schools including disclosing salaries, records, or vendor contracts less than $100,000 nor are charter schools required to have open meetings. Charter advocates argue that they lack the budget to comply with FOIA requests while open government advocates and parents want more access to information. Do you believe charters should be subject to FOIA? How would you resolve these opposing stances on charter school transparency?

I support greater transparency for publicly funded charter schools, which serve almost half of all public school students in DC.

The DC Public Charter School Board (DCPCSB) is subject to FOIA. DCPCSB collects information from the charter schools, and could potentially play a role in developing a standard for transparency and disseminating the appropriate information.

We need to better understand what charter schools need to be able to be more transparent and respond to requests. Is the need for an additional staff member, training for someone currently on staff who could do it but doesn’t have the skill-set, or something else? We can strike a balance between the information that is needed to help the public understand public charter school operations, and still provide the space for public charter schools to do what they need to do to focus on the students and be successful.

In the long-term, transparency is part of the solution to identifying issues, but it does not necessarily lead to improvements. Solutions that tackle the root issues still need to be implemented, sustained, and managed.

HOUSING

4. DC4D has joined the Reclaim Rent Control coalition, which advocates for expanding rent control. Do you support their platform in its entirety? If not, explain where you differ. What other ideas would you propose to help residents who are negatively impacted by rising rents?

I support the three goals of the Reclaim Rent Control platform: to expand rent control to protect more tenants, to close loopholes in the existing law to stop the loss of affordable housing, and to keep rent-controlled housing affordable for current tenants.

Additionally, with the growing number of new developments in DC, tenants need:

Greater education and awareness for tenants on the Tenant Opportunity to Purchase Act (TOPA). An oversight structure would be beneficial, such as a tracking system to document when a landlord looking to sell sends the TOPA notice to affected tenants. This will help make sure tenants have enough time to review. If English is not the tenants' first language, they should be given the materials in their native language.

5. Our public housing tenants are facing a serious crisis, and we have little confidence in the DC Housing Authority’s plan to “reposition” public housing. DC4D has endorsed a petition to DCHA with recommendations on protecting tenants. Have you signed the petition; if not, why not? What other policies would you propose to protect and/or expand public housing and housing for very low-income residents in DC?

I support protecting public housing tenants’ rights and the rights of very low income residents. I absolutely agree with the need for racial justice, disability justice, and making sure that our seniors can truly age in place. I have reviewed the Petition site, and would like to request more time to read through the full recommendations.

GOVERNMENT/ETHICS

6. Are you participating in Fair Elections? If not, why not? What other ideas would you propose to limit the influence of money in politics?

I am participating in the DC Fair Elections Program. 

We may also want to reconsider term limits. In developing a limit, we need to strike a balance in giving an elected official enough time to develop policies and making an impact, and documenting and transitioning institutional knowledge.

7. Do you support abolishing Constituent Service Funds and replacing them with appropriated funds to serve resident needs? If not, explain.

Constituent Service Funds were designed to service resident needs. I don't believe the immediate step is to abolish Constituent Service Funds. However, we must make sure they're put to the appropriate uses, and serving residents’ needs.

8. Will you commit to vote for Vote16DC?

I commit to vote for Vote16DC.

9. Since DC frequently has races with many candidates in which the winner gets far less than a majority of the votes, would you support implementing ranked choice voting or some other method to ensure some level of majority support?

I support rank choice voting.

10. What role the does DC Council play during the Ballot initiative process?

As the Campaign Finance Amendment Act of 2001 states, DC Council may not expend taxpayer funds or use public resources to advocate for the passage or defeat of an initiative.

Where DC Council disagrees with initaitives that qualify for the ballot and are subsequently voted on and passed by the people, the Council should respect the people’s vote, and, where there is disagreement, there should be discussion on a roadmap forward.

CRIMINAL JUSTICE

11. Do you support decriminalizing sex work? If not, explain how you would reduce the violence and exploitation currently suffered by these workers?

I support the decriminalization of sex work.

12. Do you support ending stop and frisk in DC?

I support ending stop and frisk in DC.

13. What public health policy ideas would you propose to reduce violence in DC?

The highest percentage of arrests in DC is related to robbery and theft. To promote a public health approach, we have to get to the root of the issue.

  • We should consider bringing back trade schools. Part of the root issue is that at risk youth do not have access to the necessary resources consistently to succeed and support themselves.
  • We should support minority businesses so that minority children can see and relate to examples of success.
  • We should mandate the MPD to release “stop-and-frisk” data and create an standardized program (at least to start) on community policing.
  • We should support advocacy group and/or nonprofits that work for racial justice.

IMMIGRATION

14. Do you support legislation to prohibit the District from cooperating with federal immigration agencies, including by complying with detainer requests, absent a judicial warrant or order?

        

I believe that all individuals and residents deserve to be treated with respect. I support continuing to promote bills and emergency legislation that prohibits DC from cooperating with ICE (unless provided a judicial warrant or order). We should also continue to prohibit officers in DC from asking residents about their immigration status.

In the long-term, we should fund and support non-profits that do work in the community with immigrant families and their children; work with local businesses to support job access and advancement for immigrants; and encourage and ensure language accessibility.

ENVIRONMENT

15. Given Pepco’s resistance to transitioning to clean renewable energy, do you think DC would benefit from municipally owned electricity? If not, why?

I signed the No Pepco Pledge, which means I am not taking any corporate donations, commit to disclosing meetings with Pepco and Washington Gas representatives and lobbyists, and support legislation for an economic feasibility study on the implementation of alternative utility models, such as municipalization in DC.

16. What specific legislative initiatives will you introduce in Council Period 24 to protect DC from the effects of climate change?

The Mayor signed the Clean Energy DC Ominmus Amendement Act of 2018 in January 2019. It mandates 100 percent renewable energy by 2032. The legislation includes 57 action items. It is important that we make progress on these action items, and monitor for any gaps and/or unintended consequences.

HEALTH

17. The DC Council passed the landmark “Birth to Three” legislation providing universal child care at subsidized rates; however, they failed to fully fund it. Would solutions would you propose to create sustainable funding for the program?

Part of the challenge is that there are many priorities and we need a unified vision that is consistently and clearly implemented. To sustainably fund the Birth to Three for All DC Act, we should consider the following actions:

  • Identify the top priorities, and make sure they are fully funded. The Birth to Three for All DC Act should be one of these top priorities. DC has the highest maternal mortality rate in DC; it is urgent that we provide the necessary support to resolve this critical issue. Policies and community initiatives that directly and/or indirectly support maternal and infant health should also be given priority.
  • Identify where money is not necessary and not a priority for the fiscal year, and allocate to the Birth to Three for All DC Act.

BACKGROUND

18. (Ward 2 challengers only) Are you committed to removing the incumbent from office? If yes, what criteria should be used to determine who is the best candidate to challenge him?

I am committed, and believe the best candidate is one who listens to residents, is a collaborator, and is committed to transparency and accountability.

19. (Challengers only) As a first-year legislator, you won’t be allowed to chair a committee but will be able to express preferences for which committees you will sit on. Which committee is your top preference and why?

Committee on Health: I am passionate about health care, and access to health care for all. I have spent the last 10 years in the health care field, and have worked across the public, commercial, and non-profit sectors. 

20. Briefly explain what systems you have or would put in place to get feedback from your constituents on policy?

If elected as Councilwoman, I would focus on the following in order to get feedback from constituents on policy:

  • Transparency: Provide updates to the public about meetings that take place, major next steps, and decisions made.
  • Engagement: Work across organizations, participate in community meetings, and collaborate with community leaders to develop solutions.
  • Accessibility: Constituents will have access to my staff and myself. I will, for example, host open office hours, hold meetings at business establishments and especially small businesses, and attend community meetings.
